Is the land still not yours after registry? 😱 Supreme Court New Order 2026 | Property Law Explained

The headline matches this episode’s YouTube video. Guest: Hemant Kaushik — experience in property, civil, and corporate law. Host: Sarvesh Mishra.

Detailed notes — about a 25-minute read · Updated: 25 April 2026

In short

  • True title to land comes from a registered chain and the law — not from revenue records alone.
  • Daughters’ share now applies to both farmland and housing — selling without their rights is risky.
  • Documents, utility-bill evidence, and long undisputed possession can become durable arguments in court.

Not legal advice: This article is a literary compilation of the conversation on The Sarvesh Mishra Show. For individual matters, consult a qualified lawyer.

Land in India: emotion, money, and paperwork

At the start of the episode Sarvesh Mishra notes that India is a country of villages — millions of families depend entirely on land. Yet missing documents, pressure from relatives, and a “we’ll sort it later” mindset let disputes run for generations. Hemant Kaushik’s view is that no one is above the law — but the law must be proved with evidence, and that is where ordinary people often slip.

Unregistered documents after 2011 and “informal papers”

Hemant’s first big message: under Supreme Court direction, any unregistered document relating to property is not considered sufficient to establish title. Before 2011, registration and possession often proceeded on power of attorney or informal papers; doing that now carries much higher risk. Delaying registration to save tax can cost more than lakhs or crores spent later.

The simple message: whether fifty square yards or a hundred, get a registered title so the registrar can cross-check who has rights recorded for that plot.

“My name is on khasra–khatouni, so I own it” — not the whole truth

In villages it is commonly believed that whoever’s name appears on khasra–khatouni is the owner. Hemant Kaushik clarifies that this record is mainly for revenue and administration — subsidies, crops, patwari maps, and so on. In cities a similar process is often called mutation. Courts have held in many decisions that mutation alone does not confer title. In disputes, title is traced through a legal chain of heirs from father and grandfather down to the present.

Ancestral land, possession, and “chain of incidents”

Where old registered papers do not exist, courts and administration try to establish truth through a chain of incidents: who cultivated for decades, whose name is on electricity and water bills, who benefited from government schemes, who was on record during consolidation or disasters. All of this can count as substantial evidence.

Adverse possession: The discussion mentions principles such as uninterrupted possession on private land for twelve years; for government land, duration and conditions may differ (thirty years is mentioned in conversation). Everything depends on the facts of the case — legal advice is essential.

If land is lost to consolidation or a government project and paperwork is weak, the same historical evidence supports claims for compensation.

Daughters’ share, sale, and the buyer’s duty

Citing important Supreme Court interpretation around 2005, Hemant explains that daughters’ rights are no longer limited to the house — they extend to agricultural land as well, with equal share often discussed. If there are four children and land is sold while suppressing two heirs’ rights, such a transaction may be null and void. If a sister later goes to court, the buyer’s title can also be at risk.

The law expects the buyer to know how many heirs there are — “I didn’t know the law” is usually not a defence. If a sister voluntarily gives up her share, a formal document such as a registered relinquishment deed is required; oral “we already gave it” cannot be relied on.

If in-laws pressure after relinquishment, reclaiming is hard once a registered relinquishment exists; if relinquishment was under pressure and that is proved, the situation may differ — it depends on the facts.

A sister’s right in a joint house — what can courts do?

The discussion covers cases where brothers say “this is our family home” and a sister demands her share. Referring to Supreme Court examples, options such as renting out, sale, or auction can fall within the law — “no one is above the law.”

Parents gave a gift; now relations have soured

The Senior Citizens Welfare Act, 2007 is mentioned: if property was obtained by influencing elders or by abandoning duty, relief can be sought at the appropriate forum. On the other hand, a registered will while alive can set out future division; a will needs two witnesses from outside the family. A registered will involves government fees and brings clarity in disputes.

If registration is in the father’s name and division is needed after him, formal routes such as a succession certificate are discussed — the state may be joined as a party and fees (for example a percentage of property value) may apply; this is the cost of “clearing the papers,” or large deals stall.

Can the government still take land after registration?

Hemant explains that the legal framework also serves state interests; acquisition can happen for development projects, but due process and compensation claims are strengthened with registration and evidence. Compensation depends on local circle rates and valuation.

Empty plots — where do the worst fights happen?

“Sarvesh ji, most fights are over empty plots” — hundreds of bighas showing on one khasra while real boundaries stay unclear is a common tragedy. The advice: get boundaries spelled out in site plan / position documents; electricity meters, water connections, boring receipts, delivery slips for construction material — all become evidence over time.

Buying commercial space from a builder — what to check?

Whether someone buys a two-crore office or a developer builds a hundred acres, the title chain (farmer to government, government to authority, map, approvals) should be examined with the same depth. In the end a separate registration for your portion is solid proof — do not stop at “agreement to sell” or a booking slip alone. Keep RERA and local rules in mind.

Three cautions (for the middle class)

  1. Professional title search: As banks do before a mortgage, buyers should commission their own search.
  2. Buy less but buy clean: Title must be clear and registration certain.
  3. Register the same day: Do not fall for “informal paper first, name later”; payment, possession, and registration should be one plan.

Closing message

Sarvesh Mishra closes by saying that the wish to “build something in one’s name” attracts greed and traps — keep your reasoning as your measure. Hemant Kaushik’s effort is to make legal language understandable to ordinary people so generations are not lost in endless litigation.
