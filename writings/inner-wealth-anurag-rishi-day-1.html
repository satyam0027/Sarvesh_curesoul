The Inner Wealth · Day 1 of 7 · Guest: Anurag Rishi

Day 1: Mind VS Subconscious — you are not the master of your thoughts! (The Dark Truth 2026)

The headline matches this YouTube episode. Host: Sarvesh Mishra.

In three lines

  • Much of life is run by subconscious patterns — it feels like “we” are driving.
  • Thoughts arise from memory + conditioning + unconscious state; choice through awareness is still possible.
  • Breath, witness, deep slow breath — three small practices; ten quiet minutes a day for children.

This article is a structured summary of The Inner Wealth — Day 1 conversation. Not medical or personal counselling.

Question: are we living our life or a life scripted by others’ ideas?

Sarvesh Mishra’s first question: is a person really living their own life, or running on a “script” built from society, family, fear, and religion — creating blockages in life? Anurag Rishi suggests understanding on three levels: science (neuro / subconscious), psychology (memory and conditioning), and subconscious emotional state (old impressions, anger, grief, and so on).

Zero to seven years: theta and “downloads”

The talk briefly introduces brain waves (beta, alpha, theta). A young child’s state can be highly suggestible — what is told may store as belief without deep questioning. From the womb through childhood the body “downloads” breath, temperature, heart rate, and more, also held in the subconscious. Anurag stresses: whatever messages are given at that age — even if spoken out of fear — become beliefs.

Ninety-five percent subconscious and the illusion of “my thought”

In scientific language, much of life is influenced by the subconscious. It feels like “this is my thought,” but underneath it may be coming from stored patterns. From psychology, thoughts and decisions tie to three sources: memory (like 2+2 = 4 — instant, no new process), conditioning (like “Switzerland is heaven on earth” — accepted without going), and unconscious state (like shouting at the child first when glass breaks, or checking safety first — from old emotional stores).

Is the whole of life “in control”?

Short answer: not the way we usually think. Still, choice can connect with awareness. The discussion notes that when the brain “takes” a decision or thought, a rough decision may already have occurred milliseconds earlier — yet the conscious difference is that a thought arising and grasping that thought are not the same; the small gap in between is where awareness can enter.

When someone fails, suffers, and blames government, society, friends, or God, often responsibility is being displaced. A child who practised small choices and responsibility can stand on “that was my choice” even under heavy pressure.

Overthinking and “watching thoughts”

Against fatigue, anxiety, and depression there is often a loop on one topic. Referencing the Buddha: watch your thoughts — because what appears is not “I”; the watcher is separate. When you see a thought as an object, the split between subject and witness becomes clear.

Breath, lifespan, and “life versus breaths”

A comparison: a tortoise may live centuries with few breaths per minute; a dog with fast breathing lives fewer years. Human rapid breathing and cellular ageing are linked in modern body science — the message: breaths are “given”; how we live connects to them. During deep breath thoughts stay quieter because attention rests on breath command, not autopilot.

Parents: brainstorm, small decisions, responsibility

Give children not only instructions — first brainstorm with “what do you think?” Let them choose between two shirts so capacities like the prefrontal cortex develop. Small responsibilities (clearing a plate, filling water, simple tasks) — so failure feels like “my decision” instead of blaming others. Without both responsibility and choice, life’s “slaps” feel heavier when they step outside.

Three practices: breath, watching thoughts, deep breath

1) Witness the breath: several times a day, for a short while only watch breath — nose, throat, lungs, down to the navel. This is said to bring presence and quiet mental noise.

2) Watch thoughts: hinting at the Buddha — “what I can see is not I.” For ten seconds ask: in which part of the brain are thoughts rising? In searching, thoughts often vanish because the mind does only one deep task at a time.

3) Deep slow breath: natural rate versus today’s fast breathing; slow breath brings a sense of calm and control at a cellular level. Four to five minutes of deep breath after handing a question to consciousness / the universe — the “answer” may come not from outside but as inner clarity.

“First know; do not believe blindly”

Quoting Acharya Rajneesh (Osho): do not believe instantly what is said — first know; after knowing, the same point may still feel true. If you believe without knowing, thoughts and decisions are no longer “yours” — they run on outside influence. In awareness you can reflect on every faith and opinion and reach your own conclusion; others are not “wrong” — their conditioning may differ — an attitude like “hate the sin, not the sinner.”

Religion, ritual, and science

From his journey Anurag notes that people often grab ritual and drop the science-aligned sound / vibration behind it. Mantra sound and vibration are said to work at the cellular level — performing only custom without understanding is different. Questioning a custom like throwing a coin in a river: does the river need money, or did we catch the form and miss the essence?

On study: even after much reading knowledge can “sit behind” you without integration — witness and breath practices may help bridge that gap. One suggestion for children is repeated: ten minutes daily, eyes closed, quiet — the habit of witnessing can colour a whole life.
